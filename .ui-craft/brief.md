## 1. Product purpose

Present Pakawat Smutkun’s product-engineering work as a technical atlas of business systems, making each system’s workflow, scale, and outcome immediately understandable.

## 2. Primary user

Hiring managers, technical leads, and recruiters scanning on laptop or mobile for one to three minutes to assess product judgment, engineering breadth, and delivery scale.

## 3. Principles

1. **Product evidence over decorative interaction.** If a visual does not explain the work, remove it.
2. **The system is the story.** Show interfaces, workflows, and relationships instead of relying on skill claims.
3. **Scan first, inspect second.** Outcomes and scale must register quickly; details appear through annotations and focus.
4. **Mechanical, not theatrical.** Use precise depth and immediate feedback without novelty motion.
5. **Every artifact earns its label.** All visible content must be specific to a real project, role, capability, or action.

## 4. Success metric

Within 30 seconds, a visitor can identify Pakawat as a full-stack product engineer, understand the scale of DMMS, and find a relevant prototype or resume action.

## 5. Out of scope

- Generic 3D controls, hardware toys, or decorative objects
- Orbit controls, perspective spinning, and scroll-jacking
- Fake diagnostics, latency values, audio feedback, or decorative meters
- Continuous WebGL rendering
- Redesigning the linked prototype sites

## 6. Learned constraints

- **2026-07-17** — Use bird’s-eye product flat lays across the portfolio, never generic 3D hardware props. *Why:* generic hardware makes the work feel like a useless toy and obscures the product evidence.
- **2026-07-17** — Product flat lays replace text-heavy cards and tables; retain only concise annotations, outcomes, and actions. *Why:* the visual should carry evidence rather than decorate duplicated prose.
